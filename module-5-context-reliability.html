Section 5.1

Manage conversation context to preserve critical information across long interactions

Task Statement 5.1

Long conversations lose things — and they lose the specific things first. Amounts become “a refund,” dates become “recently,” and the customer’s exact expectation becomes a vague sense of dissatisfaction. This section is about protecting the facts that matter.

What & why

Three failure modes to know. Progressive summarization condenses numerical values, percentages, dates, and customer-stated expectations into vague summaries — the precise details are exactly what gets lost. The “lost in the middle” effect means models reliably process information at the beginning and end of long inputs but may omit findings from the middle. And tool results accumulate, consuming tokens disproportionately to their relevance — 40+ fields per order lookup when 5 matter. The countermeasure to the first is a persistent “case facts” block carried in each prompt, outside the summarized history.

Knowledge points
  • Progressive summarization risks: condensing numerical values, percentages, dates, and customer-stated expectations into vague summaries.
  • The “lost in the middle” effect: models reliably process the beginning and end of long inputs but may omit middle sections.
  • Tool results accumulate and consume tokens disproportionately to their relevance (40+ fields per lookup when only 5 are relevant).
  • The importance of passing complete conversation history in subsequent API requests to maintain coherence.
In practice
  • Extract transactional facts into a persistent “case facts” block (amounts, dates, order numbers, statuses) included in each prompt, outside summarized history.
  • Trim verbose tool outputs to relevant fields before they accumulate.
  • Place key findings at the beginning of aggregated inputs and organize details with explicit section headers, mitigating position effects.
  • Require subagents to include metadata (dates, source locations, methodological context) in structured outputs for accurate downstream synthesis.
  • Modify upstream agents to return structured data rather than verbose content when downstream agents have limited context budgets.
Anti-patterns the exam tests
  • Relying on progressive summarization to carry exact figures — amounts and dates are precisely what it degrades.
  • Burying key findings in the middle of a long aggregated input — that's where omission happens.
  • Letting raw 40-field tool results pile up in context instead of trimming to what's relevant.
Exam lens “The agent forgot the refund amount after a long conversation” → a case-facts block carried outside the summarized history, not a better summarizer. “Findings from the middle of the report are missing” → lost-in-the-middle; put key findings at the start and use explicit headers. Context bloat from tool results → trim to relevant fields.

Section 5.2

Design effective escalation and ambiguity resolution patterns

Task Statement 5.2
🔒
This section is locked.

Go to Section 5.1

Knowing when to hand off to a human is a design decision, not a vibe. The traps here are seductive: escalating because the customer sounds upset, or because the model says it isn’t confident. Neither correlates with whether a human is actually needed.

What & why

There are three legitimate escalation triggers: the customer explicitly asks for a human, the policy has a gap or exception (not merely a complex case), or the agent can’t make meaningful progress. What are not reliable triggers: sentiment and self-reported confidence scores — neither is a good proxy for actual case complexity. A frustrated customer with a simple problem should get it solved, not shunted to a queue. But an explicit request for a human is honored immediately, without first attempting an investigation. And when a lookup returns multiple matches, the agent asks for another identifier rather than guessing heuristically.

Knowledge points
  • Appropriate triggers: customer requests a human, policy exceptions/gaps (not just complex cases), inability to make meaningful progress.
  • The distinction between escalating immediately on explicit demand versus offering to resolve when the issue is straightforward.
  • Why sentiment-based escalation and self-reported confidence scores are unreliable proxies for actual case complexity.
  • How multiple customer matches require clarification (requesting additional identifiers) rather than heuristic selection.
In practice
  • Add explicit escalation criteria with few-shot examples to the system prompt showing when to escalate versus resolve autonomously.
  • Honor explicit requests for a human immediately, without first attempting investigation.
  • Acknowledge frustration while offering resolution when the issue is within capability — escalating only if the customer reiterates.
  • Escalate when policy is ambiguous or silent on the request (competitor price matching when policy only addresses own-site adjustments).
  • Instruct the agent to ask for additional identifiers on multiple matches rather than selecting heuristically.
Anti-patterns the exam tests
  • Sentiment-triggered escalation — an angry customer with a simple issue doesn't need a human, they need their issue fixed.
  • Confidence-score-triggered escalation — self-reported confidence isn't a reliable complexity signal.
  • Investigating first when the customer explicitly asked for a human — honor it immediately.
  • Picking the most likely match heuristically when a lookup is ambiguous — ask for another identifier.
  • Escalating merely because a case is complex — complexity isn't a policy gap.
Exam lens Sort the triggers cleanly: explicit request, policy gap, no progress are real; sentiment and self-reported confidence are the named unreliable proxies and appear as distractors. “Customer is angry but the issue is simple” → acknowledge and resolve, escalate only if they insist. “Customer says get me a human” → immediately, no investigation first.

Section 5.3

Implement error propagation strategies across multi-agent systems

Task Statement 5.3
🔒
This section is locked.

Complete the knowledge check in Section 5.2 to unlock it.

Go to Section 5.2

When a subagent fails, what the coordinator learns determines what it can do about it. “Search unavailable” tells it nothing. Structured error context — what failed, what was tried, what came back anyway — lets it recover intelligently.

What & why

Structured error context (failure type, attempted query, partial results, alternative approaches) is what enables intelligent coordinator recovery. Two anti-patterns sit at the extremes: silently suppressing errors (returning empty results as success) hides the problem, and terminating the entire workflow on a single failure throws away everything else that worked. Both are wrong. And the recurring distinction from Module 2 returns here: an access failure (a timeout needing a retry decision) is not a valid empty result (a successful query with no matches). Conflating them makes the coordinator retry things that already worked, or treat real failures as data.

Knowledge points
  • Structured error context (failure type, attempted query, partial results, alternative approaches) enables intelligent coordinator recovery.
  • The distinction between access failures (timeouts needing retry decisions) and valid empty results (successful queries with no matches).
  • Why generic error statuses (“search unavailable”) hide valuable context from the coordinator.
  • Why silently suppressing errors and terminating entire workflows on single failures are both anti-patterns.
In practice
  • Return structured error context: failure type, what was attempted, partial results, potential alternatives.
  • Distinguish access failures from valid empty results in reporting so the coordinator decides appropriately.
  • Have subagents implement local recovery for transient failures, propagating only what they can't resolve — with what was attempted and any partial results.
  • Structure synthesis output with coverage annotations indicating which findings are well-supported and which topic areas have gaps from unavailable sources.
Anti-patterns the exam tests
  • Generic error statuses that strip the context the coordinator needs to recover.
  • Silently returning empty results as success when a source actually failed — the gap becomes invisible.
  • Terminating the whole workflow because one subagent failed — discards all the work that succeeded.
  • Presenting synthesis without coverage annotations, so gaps from unavailable sources look like genuine absence of findings.
Exam lens Both extremes are wrong: swallow the error and kill the workflow are named anti-patterns, and they'll both appear as options. The right answer returns structured context with partial results so the coordinator decides. And watch the empty-vs-failure distinction — it recurs across Modules 2 and 5 because it's heavily tested.

Section 5.4

Manage context effectively in large codebase exploration

Task Statement 5.4
🔒
This section is locked.

Complete the knowledge check in Section 5.3 to unlock it.

Go to Section 5.3

Extended exploration sessions degrade in a specific, recognizable way: the agent stops citing the classes it actually found and starts describing "typical patterns." That's the tell — and there are concrete countermeasures.

What & why

Context degradation in long sessions shows up as inconsistent answers and references to typical patterns rather than the specific classes discovered earlier. The countermeasures: scratchpad files that persist key findings across context boundaries; subagent delegation to isolate verbose exploration while the main agent coordinates high-level understanding; and structured state persistence for crash recovery, where each agent exports state to a known location and the coordinator loads a manifest on resume. /compact reduces context usage when discovery output fills the window.

Knowledge points
  • Context degradation in extended sessions: inconsistent answers, referencing “typical patterns” rather than specific classes discovered earlier.
  • The role of scratchpad files for persisting key findings across context boundaries.
  • Subagent delegation for isolating verbose exploration output while the main agent coordinates.
  • Structured state persistence for crash recovery: agents export state to a known location; the coordinator loads a manifest on resume.
In practice
  • Spawn subagents to investigate specific questions (“find all test files,” “trace refund flow dependencies”) while the main agent preserves high-level coordination.
  • Have agents maintain scratchpad files recording key findings, referencing them for subsequent questions to counteract degradation.
  • Summarize key findings from one exploration phase before spawning subagents for the next, injecting summaries into initial context.
  • Design crash recovery using structured state exports (manifests) the coordinator loads on resume and injects into agent prompts.
  • Use /compact to reduce context usage during extended exploration.
Anti-patterns the exam tests
  • Ignoring the degradation signal — when answers turn generic ("typical patterns"), the context has degraded; pushing on produces worse answers.
  • Running verbose exploration in the main session rather than delegating to subagents.
  • Keeping findings only in conversation context instead of a scratchpad file that survives boundaries.
  • No state export, so a crash means re-exploring everything from scratch.
Exam lens The recognizable symptom: "the agent started referencing typical patterns instead of the specific classes it found" — that's context degradation, and the answers are scratchpad files, subagent delegation, and phase summaries. For crash recovery, the expected design is structured state exports plus a manifest the coordinator loads on resume.

Section 5.5

Design human review workflows and confidence calibration

Task Statement 5.5
🔒
This section is locked.

Complete the knowledge check in Section 5.4 to unlock it.

Go to Section 5.4

97% accuracy sounds like a system ready to automate. It might also be 99% on the easy document type and 61% on the one that matters. Aggregate metrics hide exactly the failures you most need to see.

What & why

The core risk: aggregate accuracy metrics may mask poor performance on specific document types or fields. Before reducing human review, you validate accuracy by segment — document type and field — not in aggregate. Stratified random sampling of high-confidence extractions measures ongoing error rates and detects novel error patterns that would otherwise go unnoticed. And field-level confidence scores, calibrated against labeled validation sets, let you route reviewer attention where it pays — low confidence or contradictory sources go to humans; the rest doesn't.

Knowledge points
  • The risk that aggregate accuracy metrics (97% overall) mask poor performance on specific document types or fields.
  • Stratified random sampling for measuring error rates in high-confidence extractions and detecting novel error patterns.
  • Field-level confidence scores calibrated using labeled validation sets for routing review attention.
  • The importance of validating accuracy by document type and field segment before automating high-confidence extractions.
In practice
  • Implement stratified random sampling of high-confidence extractions for ongoing error-rate measurement and novel pattern detection.
  • Analyze accuracy by document type and field to verify consistent performance across all segments before reducing human review.
  • Have models output field-level confidence scores, then calibrate review thresholds using labeled validation sets.
  • Route low-confidence or ambiguous/contradictory extractions to human review, prioritizing limited reviewer capacity.
Anti-patterns the exam tests
  • Automating based on aggregate accuracy without segment analysis — the 97% may hide a broken document type.
  • Never sampling high-confidence extractions — novel error patterns go undetected because nobody looks.
  • Uncalibrated confidence thresholds — a self-reported score means nothing until validated against labeled data.
Exam lens “97% accurate — can we drop human review?” → not until you've analyzed accuracy by document type and field. The aggregate is the trap. Expect stratified random sampling as the ongoing-measurement answer, and calibration against labeled validation sets as what makes confidence scores trustworthy enough to route on.

Section 5.6

Preserve information provenance and handle uncertainty in multi-source synthesis

Task Statement 5.6
🔒
This section is locked.

Complete the knowledge check in Section 5.5 to unlock it.

Go to Section 5.5

Synthesis is where sources go to die. Compress five agents’ findings into one report and the claims survive but their origins don’t — leaving you with confident assertions nobody can trace. Provenance has to be carried deliberately.

What & why

Source attribution is lost during summarization when findings are compressed without preserving claim-source mappings. The fix is structured claim-source mappings that the synthesis agent must preserve and merge rather than flatten. Related judgment calls: conflicting statistics from credible sources get annotated with attribution, not arbitrarily resolved by picking one; and temporal data needs publication or collection dates in structured outputs, or a 2023 figure and a 2025 figure look like a contradiction rather than a change over time. Finally, different content types should be rendered appropriately — financial data as tables, news as prose, technical findings as structured lists — rather than flattened into one uniform format.

Knowledge points
  • How source attribution is lost during summarization when findings are compressed without preserving claim-source mappings.
  • The importance of structured claim-source mappings that synthesis must preserve and merge.
  • How to handle conflicting statistics from credible sources: annotate conflicts with attribution rather than arbitrarily selecting one value.
  • Temporal data: requiring publication/collection dates to prevent temporal differences being misread as contradictions.
In practice
  • Require subagents to output structured claim-source mappings (source URLs, document names, relevant excerpts) that downstream agents preserve through synthesis.
  • Structure reports with explicit sections distinguishing well-established findings from contested ones, preserving original source characterizations and methodological context.
  • Complete document analysis with conflicting values included and explicitly annotated, letting the coordinator decide how to reconcile.
  • Require subagents to include publication or data collection dates for correct temporal interpretation.
  • Render content types appropriately — financial data as tables, news as prose, technical findings as structured lists.
Anti-patterns the exam tests
  • Summarizing findings without carrying claim-source mappings — the report makes claims nobody can trace.
  • Picking one value when credible sources conflict — annotate the conflict with attribution instead.
  • Omitting dates, so a change over time reads as a contradiction between sources.
  • Flattening every content type into uniform prose — financial data belongs in a table.
Exam lens “The final report has no citations” → structured claim-source mappings carried through synthesis, not a prompt asking for citations at the end. “Two credible sources disagree” → annotate both with attribution; picking one is the distractor. “Sources seem to contradict” → check for missing dates before assuming conflict.

Module 5 · Wrap-up

What this domain comes down to

Context Management & Reliability is what separates a demo from a system: facts that survive long conversations (5.1), escalation on real triggers rather than sentiment (5.2), errors that travel with enough context to recover from (5.3), exploration that doesn't degrade into vagueness (5.4), confidence calibrated per segment rather than trusted in aggregate (5.5), and claims that never lose their sources (5.6).

One thread ties the anti-patterns together: information degrades by default, and the specific always degrades first. Summaries lose the amounts. Middles get skipped. Errors flatten to "failed." Sources detach from claims. Aggregate metrics bury the broken segment. Every technique in this domain is a deliberate act against that drift — a case-facts block, a structured error, a claim-source mapping, a stratified sample. Reliability isn't something a system has; it's something you build in on purpose.
